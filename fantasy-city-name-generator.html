Fantasy City Name Generator: Names That Feel Like They Have Centuries Behind Them

A fantasy city name is doing different work than a character name. It doesn't describe a person — it describes a place that outlasted the people who built it, named it, and possibly destroyed it. The best fantasy city names feel like they arrived from somewhere else: a different language, a different era, a different civilization's priorities. When players see a city name on a map and immediately want to know its history, the name is doing its job.

This fantasy city name generator separates four distinct settlement types because an empire capital and a coastal trading port don't just look different — they sound different, and the naming conventions reflect that.

The Four Fantasy City Naming Traditions

  • Empire capitals carry grand Latin and Greek compound structures — formal, heavy, names that were built to be inscribed in stone. Varenthal. Osperium. Caldrevast. These cities named themselves for permanence. The name should feel like it belongs above an arch, not on a signpost.
  • Coastal cities use fluid, mercantile phonetics — names that move easily between languages, that traders from three different cultures can pronounce without argument. Selvara. Thalport. Morvenne. These cities grew around commerce, and their names grew with them — practical, pronounceable, slightly worn at the edges from use.
  • Mountain strongholds go hard and angular — consonant-heavy names that feel like the stone they were carved from. Durnholt. Grathvend. Kelvrak Keep. The name should feel immovable. These settlements didn't name themselves for visitors; they named themselves for whoever was coming to take them.
  • Ancient ruins carry half-eroded names — compounds where one element has been lost to time, names that suggest a full word that no longer exists. Vel-thar. Ossindrae. Morth-el. The incompleteness is structural. Something was there before, and the name is what survived.

What Makes a Fantasy City Name Feel Historical

Real city names accrete meaning over time — they layer founding language over conquering language over common usage until the original meaning is buried. The best fantasy city names replicate that compression. A name like Varenthal could be read as two root-words fused by centuries of use. Selvara sounds like it was shortened from something longer. That implied history is what separates a worldbuilding city name from a placeholder.

Two structural rules apply across all types. First, avoid pure description — Darktown, Stonecity, Riverport are labels, not names. Second, the name should be pronounceable but not immediately parseable. If the player can read it and instantly understand what it means, it hasn't aged enough.

Using City Names in D&D Campaigns and Fiction

For DMs, drop the city name before the city appears. A merchant mentions it in passing. It appears on a wanted poster. The name builds expectation that the reveal either confirms or subverts. For fiction writers, the city name is the first piece of worldbuilding the reader encounters — it should feel lived-in before the first sentence of description.

Quick Tips

  • Avoid pure description — Darktown and Stonecity are labels, not names. The meaning should be etymologically buried, not immediately readable.
  • Empire capital names belong above arches: Latin-Greek compounds, three syllables minimum, closed on a formal suffix built for stone inscription.
  • Coastal city names should feel worn by use — fluid, multi-language pronounceable, slightly eroded at the edges like a coin that's changed hands too many times.
  • Ruin names carry structural incompleteness — a hyphen, a truncated suffix, a compound missing its second half. The erosion is phonetic before it's visual.
  • Drop the city name before the city appears — a merchant's passing mention, a line on a wanted poster. The name builds expectation that the reveal either confirms or subverts.

Pro Tip

The best fantasy city names feel like they were shortened from something longer — that implied full name, lost to time, is the history your players will invent for themselves, which makes the world feel real before you've written a single lore document.

Frequently Asked Questions

What makes a fantasy city name feel realistic?
Implied history. Real city names layer founding language over conquering language over common usage until the original meaning is buried. A good fantasy city name replicates that compression — it should look like two root-words fused by centuries of use, or like it was shortened from something longer. If the meaning is immediately obvious, the name hasn't aged enough.
What sounds work for empire capital names?
Grand Latin and Greek compound structures — formal, heavy, built for stone inscription. Varenthal. Osperium. Caldrevast. Three syllables minimum, closed on a formal suffix. These names were chosen for permanence, not convenience. They belong above arches, not on signposts.
How do coastal city names differ from mountain stronghold names?
Coastal names are fluid and mercantile — worn slightly at the edges from use across multiple languages, pronounceable by traders from three different cultures. Selvara. Morvenne. Mountain stronghold names go hard and angular — consonant-heavy, immovable, named for whoever was coming to take them, not for visitors. Durnholt. Grathvend. One sounds like commerce; the other sounds like a siege.
How do I name ancient ruins in my fantasy world?
Build the incompleteness into the structure. Ruin names should suggest a full word that no longer exists — a compound where one element has been lost to time. Vel-thar. Ossindrae. Morth-el. The hyphen or truncated suffix signals erosion. Something was there before, and the name is what the rubble remembers.
Should fantasy city names have obvious meanings?
No — pure description kills the historical illusion. Darktown, Stonecity, Riverport are labels, not names. The meaning can be etymologically buried — a root-word from an in-world dead language, a founder's name eroded by use — but it shouldn't be immediately parseable on first read. If the player understands it instantly, it hasn't aged.
Can I use these city names for D&D homebrew campaigns?
Yes, and they're designed for that use case. Empire capitals work for major political centers, coastal names for port cities and trade hubs, mountain stronghold names for dwarven fortresses or highland keeps, ruin names for ancient dungeons and lost civilizations. Each type carries implicit worldbuilding that reduces the work of explaining context.
How many syllables should a fantasy city name have?
Two to three for most types — short enough to use in conversation, long enough to carry weight. Empire capitals can stretch to four syllables when the context demands formality. Ruin names sometimes compress to two syllables or a hyphenated fragment, the truncation itself signaling age. Avoid one-syllable city names unless the settlement is deliberately minor or the brevity is a worldbuilding detail.
