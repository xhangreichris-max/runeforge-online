Tiefling Name Generator: Infernal Heritage, Worn Without Apology

Tiefling names are an act of identity. In D&D 5e lore, tieflings are humans with infernal blood — they didn't choose the heritage, but they choose what to do with it. That choice shows up directly in naming. Some tieflings take Latinized infernal names that lean into what their bloodline is. Some adopt virtue names — abstract nouns like Despair or Ruin — as a kind of defiance, naming themselves after what others already assume about them. Both are correct. Both tell you something about the character before they speak.

This tiefling name generator covers all four naming traditions used in D&D tiefling lore, with phonetic logic for each.

The Four Tiefling Naming Traditions

  • Virtue names are the most distinctly tiefling convention — abstract nouns worn as names, sometimes ironic, sometimes sincere, always loaded. Despair. Ruin. Mercy. Spite. The choice of which virtue says as much as the name itself. A tiefling named Mercy has made a decision about how to be seen. A tiefling named Carnage has made a different one.
  • Infernal names draw from Latinized demonic phonetics — hard consonants, formal compound structures, names that sound like they belong in a contract written in a language older than Common. Akmenos. Pyraxes. Mordai. These names were often given by tiefling parents who wanted their child to own their heritage rather than hide it.
  • Hybrid names blend infernal roots with common suffixes — names that could pass in a human city but carry the infernal phonetics if you know what to listen for. Kelvras. Zorindel. Thyxara. These characters are usually navigating both worlds simultaneously.
  • Noble tiefling names carry aristocratic infernal structures — long, formal, often with a house suffix that signals old bloodline rather than street origin. Mephistaran. Belzindrel. Asmoveth. These names belong to tieflings from families that have had centuries to decide the heritage is a feature, not a flaw.

The Virtue Name Tradition: How It Actually Works

The virtue name convention is unique to tieflings in D&D and it's frequently misunderstood. It doesn't mean the name describes the character — it means the character chose a name that describes what the world already projects onto them, then decided to carry it differently. A tiefling named Despair isn't necessarily despairing. They're telling you that despair is something they've already survived. The name is armor, not confession.

Infernal Phonetics: What Makes a Tiefling Name Sound Right

Infernal names pull from Latin and invented demonic roots — hard stops, long formal vowels, compound endings that suggest something ancient and contractual. The sounds that work: -ax, -os, -ael, -dros, -veth, -imar. The sounds that don't: anything that reads as generically elvish (-iel, -wyn) or obviously Germanic (-helm, -gar). Tiefling names exist in their own phonetic register, distinct from every other D&D race, and that distinctiveness is part of the point.

Quick Tips

  • Virtue names are armor, not confession — a tiefling named Despair has survived it, not surrendered to it. The choice of which virtue is the character detail.
  • Infernal phonetics: hard stops, long formal vowels, compound endings. -ax, -os, -dros, -veth. Never elvish, never Germanic.
  • Hybrid names work for tieflings navigating both worlds — infernal roots, common suffixes, passes in a human city but the heritage is still audible.
  • Noble tiefling names don't get shortened in formal settings — four syllables minimum, and the full name signals old bloodline, not street origin.
  • A tiefling with a fully human name carries a specific tension: the name says one thing, the appearance says another. Build that gap deliberately.

Pro Tip

The most revealing tiefling character detail isn't the name itself — it's whether they chose it themselves or inherited it, because that single fact tells you how they've decided to relate to everything their bloodline means.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the different types of tiefling names in D&D 5e?
Four main traditions: virtue names (abstract nouns like Despair or Mercy worn as identity statements), infernal names (Latinized demonic phonetics from family or heritage), hybrid names (infernal roots with common suffixes for characters navigating both worlds), and noble tiefling names (long aristocratic infernal structures from old bloodlines). The Player's Handbook covers virtue and infernal names specifically.
What is a tiefling virtue name and how does it work?
A virtue name is an abstract noun — Despair, Ruin, Carnage, Mercy, Spite — worn as a personal name. It doesn't describe the character's personality; it describes what the world projects onto tieflings, reclaimed deliberately. A tiefling named Despair has already survived despair. The name is armor. The choice of which virtue tells you how the character has decided to be seen.
What sounds define infernal tiefling names?
Hard stops, long formal vowels, and compound endings that feel ancient and contractual — -ax, -os, -ael, -dros, -veth, -imar. Akmenos. Pyraxes. Mordai. Avoid elvish endings (-iel, -wyn) and Germanic endings (-helm, -gar). Tiefling infernal names exist in their own phonetic register, distinct from every other D&D race.
Can tieflings have human names?
Yes. Tieflings raised in human families often take human names, sometimes with no infernal reference at all. In play, a tiefling with a completely human name carries a different kind of tension — the name says 'I am trying to be seen as human' and the horns say something else entirely. That gap is a character detail worth building around.
What's the difference between a hybrid tiefling name and an infernal one?
Infernal names are fully rooted in demonic Latinized phonetics — formal, ancient-sounding, unambiguous in origin. Hybrid names blend those infernal roots with common suffixes that could pass in a human city: Kelvras, Zorindel, Thyxara. The infernal DNA is still audible, but the name functions in both worlds. Hybrid characters are usually the ones actively navigating the space between.
Are tiefling names gendered in D&D lore?
Loosely. Virtue names are inherently gender-neutral — Despair and Ruin don't have a gender. Infernal names have soft gender leanings in the Player's Handbook examples, but nothing prescriptive. Noble tiefling names follow the aristocratic family's conventions, which vary. In practice, pick the name that fits the character's identity choice, not a gender table.
How do I choose between a virtue name and an infernal name for my tiefling?
Ask what the character decided about their heritage. Infernal names lean into bloodline pride or acknowledgment — the character owns what they are. Virtue names are a different kind of ownership: they take what the world assumes and reframe it. A tiefling who chose their own name after leaving home almost always picks a virtue name. One named by infernal-heritage parents almost always carries an infernal name.
