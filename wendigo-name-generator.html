Wendigo Name Generator: Naming the Pop-Culture Horror Archetype

Before any naming guidance, a clarification worth stating plainly. The wendigo originates in the spiritual and cultural traditions of Algonquian-speaking peoples, including Cree, Ojibwe, and Innu nations. It carries real religious and cultural weight within those communities, and it is not a generic monster available for casual reinvention. This page does not attempt to generate names for that figure.

What this page covers instead is the pop-culture horror archetype that emerged from decades of non-Indigenous fiction — Algernon Blackwood's 1910 short story, Marvel's Wendigo character, and modern horror media like Antlers and Until Dawn. That archetype — antlered, emaciated, forest-bound, cannibalistic — has its own naming conventions built entirely within fantasy and horror fiction, separate from the source tradition. This guide sticks to that fictional archetype.

Naming Conventions for Horror-Archetype Wendigo Characters

Names for this fictional creature type tend to favor harsh, scraping consonant combinations, particularly clusters using R, K, and guttural sounds that feel difficult to pronounce smoothly. The phonetic goal is discomfort. A name that catches in the throat slightly when spoken aloud fits the archetype better than something fluid or melodic.

Many fictional treatments lean into ambiguity rather than a proper name at all, referring to the creature by title or description: the Hollow One, the Starving Thing, the Antlered Walker. When a proper name is used in fiction, it often sounds fragmented or partially forgotten, as if the name itself has decayed along with whatever the creature once was. This reflects a common horror trope: the wendigo-archetype figure as a once-human entity that lost its identity along with its humanity.

Using This Archetype in D&D Horror Campaigns

Curse of Strahd and other Gothic horror D&D content frequently feature wendigo-inspired stat blocks, typically built from existing monster templates dressed in the antlered, starving aesthetic that pop culture associates with the name. When naming this kind of NPC, the fragmented or title-based naming approach above tends to land better at the table than a fully formed proper name, since the horror often works best when the creature's identity feels incomplete or stolen rather than fully realized.

A Note on Respectful Use

If your worldbuilding wants to engage with Indigenous-rooted folklore specifically, rather than the horror-fiction archetype, the better approach is direct engagement with primary sources and, where possible, work by Indigenous authors and scholars writing about their own traditions, rather than treating the figure as raw material for a generic name generator. The naming guidance on this page is scoped intentionally to the fictional horror archetype that already exists independently in genre fiction, not to the tradition it borrowed its name from.
