Dungeon Name Generator: Names That Signal Danger Before the DM Says a Word

A dungeon name is the first piece of information players receive about a location they haven't entered yet. Everything that happens at the table before the first room description — the hesitation, the preparation, the arguments about whether to go in at all — starts with that name. Get it right and the players are already afraid. Get it wrong and the dungeon has to earn its dread from scratch, room by room, which is harder and less reliable.

This dungeon name generator separates four location types because an ancient tomb and a dark fortress don't produce the same fear, and their names shouldn't try to.

The Four Dungeon Naming Traditions

  • Ancient tombs carry death and silence sounds — names that feel like something sealed a long time ago and has been waiting. Ossindrath. The Vault of Vel'mor. Morthkael's Rest. The name should imply that whatever is inside has had a very long time to become whatever it is now. Silence is structural: no aggressive consonants, no urgency — just age and enclosure.
  • Dark fortresses use heavy, imposing names — hard consonants, closed vowels, names that feel like walls. Durgvast. The Iron Dominion. Krath'hold. These names don't imply age so much as mass. The fortress is still functioning. Something is still inside running it. The name should feel like a warning issued by whoever built it, not a memorial left by whoever destroyed it.
  • Cave systems carry hollow, echoing phonetics — open vowels and resonant consonants that suggest depth and disorientation. The Hollows of Umbren. Veltharax Depths. Ossavel Maw. These names should feel like they go further in than the players expect. The mouth is just the beginning.
  • Forgotten ruins use half-eroded names — the same structural incompleteness that defines ancient city ruins, applied to single-location dungeons. Morth-el. The Sundered Athenaeum. Vel'thas Fragment. One part of the name is missing or broken. The full name belonged to a civilization that no longer exists, and no one alive knows what it meant.

What Makes a Dungeon Name Do Its Job

Three things. First, it should be pronounceable but not immediately parseable — the players can say it without effort, but they can't easily extract meaning from it. Second, it should resist casual abbreviation. A dungeon players start calling 'the Vault' or 'the Hold' has lost half its threat. The name should be specific enough that shortening it feels wrong. Third — and this is the one most dungeon name guides skip — the name should imply a question the players can't answer yet. The Vault of Vel'mor: who is Vel'mor? The Hollows of Umbren: what is Umbren? The question is the hook.

Using Dungeon Names in D&D Campaigns

Name the dungeon before the players decide to enter it. A rumor in a tavern. A line on a bounty board. A warning from the last person who came back. 'They went into Durgvast three weeks ago. Two of them came back. Neither one will say what happened inside.' By the time the players stand at the entrance, the name already has a body count attached to it — and they put it there themselves.

Quick Tips

  • Name the dungeon before players decide to enter — rumor, warning, dead NPC's note. The name earns dread in the sessions before the door.
  • Ancient tomb names carry silence, not aggression — age and enclosure, no urgency. The threat is how long it's been waiting.
  • Fortress names feel like active warnings: hard consonants, closed vowels, something still running inside.
  • Cave system names need depth — open vowels and resonant consonants that imply the entrance is just the beginning.
  • The best dungeon names resist casual abbreviation. The moment players start calling it 'the Vault,' half the threat is gone.

Pro Tip

Every dungeon name should imply an unanswered question — who is Vel'mor, what is Umbren, what happened at Krath'hold — because the players will answer it themselves before they enter, and whatever they imagine is almost always worse than what you planned.

Frequently Asked Questions

What makes a dungeon name frightening before players enter?
Three things: it's pronounceable but not immediately parseable, it resists casual abbreviation, and it implies a question the players can't answer yet. The Vault of Vel'mor makes them ask who Vel'mor was. The Hollows of Umbren makes them ask what Umbren is. The unanswered question is the hook — and it works before the first room description.
What sounds define ancient tomb names?
Death and silence sounds — no aggressive consonants, no urgency, just age and enclosure. Ossindrath. Morthkael's Rest. The Vault of Vel'mor. The name should imply that whatever is inside has had a very long time to become whatever it is now. Tomb names don't threaten. They wait.
How do fortress dungeon names differ from tomb names?
Fortress names carry mass, not age. Hard consonants, closed vowels, names that feel like walls still standing. Durgvast. Krath'hold. The Iron Dominion. These names imply something is still inside running the place — an active threat, not a sealed one. Where tomb names feel like memorials, fortress names feel like warnings issued by whoever built them.
What phonetics work for cave system names?
Hollow, echoing sounds — open vowels and resonant consonants that suggest depth and disorientation. The Hollows of Umbren. Veltharax Depths. Ossavel Maw. The name should feel like it goes further in than expected. 'Maw' as a suffix works precisely because it implies the cave is the entrance to something else, not the destination itself.
How do I name a forgotten ruin dungeon?
Build structural incompleteness into the name. One part should be missing or broken — a truncated compound, a hyphenated fragment, a word from a dead language with no surviving translation. Morth-el. The Sundered Athenaeum. Vel'thas Fragment. The full name belonged to a civilization that no longer exists. The incompleteness signals that nobody alive knows what it originally meant.
Should a dungeon have a subtitle or just a single name?
Both work for different types. Ancient tombs and arcane ruins benefit from a possessive or qualifying subtitle — The Vault of Vel'mor, The Hollows of Umbren — because the subtitle adds the unanswered question. Fortresses and cave systems often work better as single compound names — Durgvast, Krath'hold — because the brevity implies the name is all anyone needs to know.
When should I reveal the dungeon name to players in a campaign?
Before they decide to enter — ideally two to three sessions before they arrive. A rumor. A warning. A line in a dead NPC's journal. By the time players stand at the entrance, the name should already have context they supplied themselves: assumptions, fears, second-hand accounts. The dungeon doesn't need to justify its reputation on entry if the name already built it.
