Mythical Name Generator: Naming Beings, Places, and Artifacts

Most mythical name generators stop at creatures. This one doesn't. A myth-inspired world needs names for the gods and monsters, sure, but also for the sword the hero carries, the mountain where the gods live, the lost city nobody can find anymore. This guide covers all three categories, drawing naming logic from Greek, Norse, Celtic, and Egyptian roots, and shows how to build a consistent mythical naming system for an original world rather than picking names at random from four unrelated traditions.

Naming Mythical Beings

Greek-rooted being names favor clear etymological meaning, since Greek mythology names were rarely arbitrary. Apollo means light, Nike means victory. Building a being's name from a meaningful Greek root, even an invented one, gives the name weight beyond its sound. Norse-rooted being names lean harder into phonetic harshness, reflecting a mythology built around struggle against entropy and giants: Fenrir, Yggdrasil, names that sound like they were carved rather than spoken. Celtic-rooted being names favor flowing, vowel-rich constructions tied to nature and otherworldly liminality: Brigid, Lugh, Cerridwen. Egyptian-rooted being names carry formal, ceremonial weight, often built from compound elements referencing the divine or the eternal: Anubis, Sekhmet, Thoth.

Naming Mythical Places

Place names across these traditions tend to describe function or feature rather than just sounding pretty. Greek place names often reference geography directly: Olympus essentially means "the mountain." Norse place names frequently compound two concepts — Asgard combines "Aesir" and "garden/enclosure," Valhalla combines "the slain" and "hall." Celtic place names lean into otherworldly thresholds, names for places that sit between the mortal world and something else, often tied to mist, water, or burial mounds. Egyptian place names carry religious function baked directly into the name — the Field of Reeds, the Hall of Two Truths — names that describe what happens there rather than just labeling a location.

Naming Mythical Artifacts

A mythical weapon or relic needs a name that implies its power or origin rather than just sounding cool. Greek-tradition artifact names often reference the artifact's creator or the deed it accomplished: the Aegis, the Golden Fleece. Norse-tradition artifact names favor short, hard-hitting single words that function almost like titles: Mjolnir, Gjallarhorn. Celtic-tradition artifact names lean toward poetic, slightly riddling constructions, since Celtic myth treats magic items as having their own will and history. Egyptian-tradition artifact names often pair a material or form with a divine reference — the Crook of Osiris, the Eye of Ra — structures that ground the object's power in a specific deity's domain.

Building a Consistent Mythical Naming System

The biggest mistake in original worldbuilding is mixing traditions without intention. A world with a Greek-named god, a Norse-named sword, and a Celtic-named capital city feels scattered rather than rich, unless that mixing is itself part of the lore — multiple pantheons colliding, for instance. Pick one or two root traditions for your core mythology and stay disciplined about it. If your setting has multiple cultures, assign each culture its own tradition deliberately, the way Golarion assigns Roman roots to Taldor and Egyptian roots to Osirion, rather than letting names drift randomly across your map.

Once you've picked your root traditions, build a small phonetic rule set for each: which consonant clusters appear, which vowel patterns repeat, whether compound names are common. This rule set, even an informal one in your own head, keeps every name you generate afterward feeling like it belongs to the same world rather than reading like four separate name generators stapled together.

Use Cases

Worldbuilding projects benefit most directly from this kind of system, since consistent naming across beings, places, and artifacts is one of the fastest ways to make an original setting feel lived-in rather than invented on the spot. Fantasy novelists need this same consistency across a manuscript — readers notice when naming logic breaks down partway through a book. RPG lore documents, whether for a homebrew tabletop setting or a published sourcebook, hold together better when every name traces back to a clear, intentional root tradition rather than a grab bag of whatever sounded good that day.

  • Artifact names work best when they imply power or origin rather than just sounding cool
  • Assign each culture in a multi-culture setting its own distinct tradition, the way real-world myth systems vary by region
